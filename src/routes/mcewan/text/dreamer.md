The sun had not yet risen on that Autumn morning. Mitchell was awoken by the clattering of a letter through his door. The sound reverberated through the slightly damp air of his one room flat. He dragged himself out of bed. There was no need to turn the lights on — the residual glow of the city cut through his curtains. He heard some sirens in the streets below. The time was seven in the morning.
Opening the envelope he found a short letter from his employer which detailed the premature termination of his contract with the company. The letter detailed the various reasons for this decision, yet he paid no attention to this. He found some clothes that appeared clean enough and grabbed a breakfast bar from the cupboard. He did not bother to lock the door after leaving the flat.
He stepped onto the elevator on the twenty-first floor, and a minute later arrived at the ground floor lobby. Immediately he was greeted by the sound of advertisements blaring throughout the streets; the glass door intended to keep the lobby quiet had been shattered some years ago.
It was busy on the roads even at that early hour. It was busy at all hours. Mitchell wondered to himself as to where all these people were going, he pitied them even — the thought of spending so much of his life stuck in traffic, truly unfathomable.
Of course, were he still employed he would have been supposed to have been on a bus by now travelling across the city to sit at his desk and write data prompts all day long. He would have been watching the small ticker in the corner of his monitor track the percentage improvements he was making to the model: 0.0001%, 0.0002%, and so on.
While he was employed that was supposed to have been his week, but it seldom was. He would only work once or twice a month — earning himself just enough money to buy two energy bars each evening; one to eat for dinner, and one to eat in the morning. As with every other worker, his real contribution to the company’s product was so low that it hardly made a difference as to whether he showed up or not. Even so, it was only a matter of time until the system caught on and ousted him.
He came to the boulevard, one of several that had been recently constructed throughout the city. A sign hung over its entrance: EverLucid Developments, and on either side were large digital billboards displaying headsets against bright white backgrounds. There were no words on either of these screens, as the small symbol of a sideways crescent moon was all that was needed to communicate their meaning.
He walked to the building at the centre of the boulevard. The same symbol was adorned in the very sandstone above its entrance, its presence like a great eye looming over visitors. Inside was a large white room, with cool air and quiet ambience — almost like that of a hospital lobby. This was despite the large crowd, which was huddled in a mass around the main reception.
Mitchell retrieved a small purple card from his pocket and held it in the air.
“Excuse me, sorry, priority pass.” He raised his voice slightly and the crowd opened to reluctantly let him through.
The receptionist recognised him instantly, and gave him a quick wave as he approached.
“Hey Mitch, how you doing?”
“Well, the bots finally saw what I was up to at work. Doesn’t matter, I’ll just nab the same thing somewhere else.”
“Man, what are you on now? Job four? Five?”
“Six probably.”
“Sheesh, well anyway what’ll it be? Just your usual?”
“Yeah, twelve hours. I’ll grab my dinner at the end.”
“Sure thing.” The receptionist hit a few keys at his computer, and printed a small ticket which he handed to Mitchell. “Booth five, twelve hours.”
“Thanks.”
“No problem, enjoy your stay with EverLucid.”
Mitchell wormed his way back out of the crowd and made for the booth. It was even quieter in the hallways, with only the soft whirr of computer fans filling the air. Doors lined the sides, each labelled with large bold numbers. He scanned his ticket at the door, at which point he noticed the price had increased by a few pence.
The booth was just like the rest of the building. Pure white walls with rounded corners, and an almost seamlessly integrated air vent in the ceiling. In the centre of the booth was a bed that was raised and shaped in a clinical fashion, below which was a circular strip of light that illuminated the room in a slightly warm glow. Above the headrest was a sleek white headset attached with an adjustable mount.
He laid himself down on the bed, and hinged the headset over his eyes. The lights dimmed and shifted to a purple hue. He closed his eyes, and fell instantly into a deep sleep.

Mitchell did not dream. He awoke once again in his flat. Checking his phone — it read seven in the morning.
The door handle turned, and in stepped a tall thin man. He wore a long white lab coat which was buttoned from the waist up, along with white boots, and a pair of white trousers. A plastic helmet covered his head, yet it was shaped so closely to its contours that one might have thought the man himself to have a plastic head. It too was a matte white.
“What a miserable set of scenes that was,” the man said with a sharp and nasal voice. “And of course that made you the perfect customer.”
The man approached slowly, his footsteps ringing loudly against the hard laminate floor, and Mitchell realised that the city had gone silent. He looked up in horror at the white figure.
“You’re dreaming, Mitchell. Everything you just experienced happened three months ago, you’re only reliving the memory.”
The questions poured through Mitchell’s brain.
“You are currently under legal custody of the EverLucid Corporation. Your real body is in a secure facility in an undisclosed location, where you are currently strapped into a headset which is currently giving you this exact dream.”
“Why am I here?”
The man knelt by his bedside. He spoke drily. “You are a terrorist Mitchell. Of course you don’t know that right now — we’ve put those memories away for the moment — but nonetheless that is what you are and I’m here to find out how that happened.”
“You’re an EverLucid employee?”
The room shifted. The walls folded outwards and suddenly the two of them were in the boulevard — the EverLucid building towering above.
“Yes, Mitchell,” the man continued, “and for the duration of this interrogation you may call me K.”
K snapped his fingers and time froze around them.
“So, you lost your job — no big deal to you, you were intent on simply finding a new one. Not exactly an uncommon way of life in these times, far from uncommon actually. Yet something happened this time around and you never did get that new job, at least not as we might have expected.”  The world shifted again, and the two of them were placed back inside the small white booth. “So let’s take a look at what that might have been, shall we?”
The lights flickered, and suddenly Mitchell was back on the bed. He was not visibly strapped in, yet he could feel the tightening of buckles around his body and limbs. White faded to purple once more, and again the headset descended upon him — sending him further into the dream.

He felt a soft breeze against his cheeks, and his hand brushed against tall grass. Mitchell opened his eyes to find himself at the centre of a large field. A dirt path stretched ahead of him. His phone buzzed in his pocket, he opened it to see a message from an unlabelled number that simply read: “9:00, don’t be late.”
He tapped the message and checked back through the conversation, discovering that this unknown person was offering him a job — and they had requested to meet in the forest. The time was twelve minutes to nine.
He continued down the path, crossing the threshold into woodland. It was an overcast day, and a constant dampness permeated the air. The path terminated in a small clearing — at the centre of which stood a woman. She was dressed in a long black coat.
“Are you Mitchell?” She called out upon noticing him.
“Yeah, are you the weird number on my phone that’s offering me a job?”
The woman put her hand out, Mitch shook it hesitantly. “Yes, my name is Siobhan. Come with me.”
He was led to a small wooden cabin. Jutting out from the roof was a tall metal truss that held an antenna at its top. Soibhan brought him inside, revealing the cabin to be packed with black boxes covered in dials and fluorescent screens.
She pointed to one screen in particular. It displayed an erratic waveform that was punctuated by sudden bursts of loudness.
“The job is simple,” she explained, using a pencil and paper to demonstrate, “I need you to sit here and record the time of each of these blips on this sheet.”
“That’s it?” Mitchell asked.
“That’s it. Give it a go for a few hours, I’ll come back at midday. If I’m happy with the results you can have the job.”
Siobhan promptly left without elaborating further. Mitchell sat and recorded the data as he was shown. The work was monotonous, yet he enjoyed the sounds of the forest around him. It had been many years since he had left the city.

Siobhan returned exactly three hours later. She inspected the roughly four sheets Mitchell had filled in that time.
“Good.” She folded them up and stuffed them in her pocket. “You want the job?”
“Yeah?”
“It’s yours then. Let's get you acquainted with the team.”
Mitchell expressed some confusion — the lack of a contract in particular — yet Siobhan simply brushed off his concerns. They walked out the door together, and made their way deeper into the woods.
