The digital clock positioned at the head of the bus read five minutes past midnight. There were two people on the bus that night. The driver was accustomed to such shifts, yet nonetheless a weariness persisted upon him. Tonight however he was a content man, for the lone passenger had tipped him substantially.
The passenger sat at the back of the bus in the single seat that looked directly down the aisle. Wedged between the seats of the row in front of him was a large suitcase, upon which he had placed a laptop. The man scrolled absent mindedly through various articles, his leg twitched restlessly yet he was not anxious - he simply disliked remaining seated for a prolonged period of time, and he yearned for his upright desk.
It was a still night. This particular region of the city saw little life at these hours. At some point — perhaps an hour or thirty minutes ago — a threshold had been crossed, and where formerly there were youths and bars and clubs, there were now rows of old buildings where bankers slept soundly on the top floors.
The bus came to a stop.
“East campus!” The driver called out.
The passenger closed his laptop and pushed the suitcase free. He stood up and removed the unused plane pillow that was wrapped around his neck, clipping it to his backpack (which he had also worn for the duration of the trip). He wore poorly fitted jeans and a grey shirt. Trundling his way to the front of the bus he thanked the driver before stepping off.

The man walked towards the gate ahead of him. It was inconspicuous, placed between two white concrete pillars with only a small metal plaque to denote the institution it belonged to. The building cantilevered over him, its slanted bare walls and black windows sitting somewhere in-between brutalism and modernism, though lacking the thought provoking qualities of either. He pulled his phone from his pocket and deftly typed out a message. A few moments later a buzzer sounded, and he was able to push the gate open and let himself inside.
Crossing through a short tunnel that cut through the bleak facade, he came to a large square courtyard. A multi-tiered fountain stood at the centre of the construction, its delicate sculptures and careful proportions might have made an impression; however it was dark, the fountain itself was turned off, and the man to whom it was bearing witness — simply did not care.
The courtyard was an architecturally sealed region. The outer walls sloped inwards, and were it not for the effects of light pollution blotting out the stars, then one might have been forgiven for thinking themselves in the middle of the countryside, far away from the city.
Only a single light was illuminated then, a small bulb that sat above an insignificant looking door on the other side of the grass. The man made his way to the door, and entered the building.

“This is it!” An agitated voice could be heard echoing down the dimly lit hallway. “They’re going to pull our funding. In nine hours — less than nine hours — the director is going to walk into this room and see that we have achieved practically nothing in four months, and he’s going to pull the plug!”
The man stepped into the laboratory. Fluorescent lights stung his eyes, yet as he adjusted to the conditions he saw a small group of people come into view. There was one man with his head against a desk, presumably sleeping, with his oily hair drooped over his face, wearing clothes that appeared as though they had not been washed in several days. Another sat at a counter, struggling to stay awake — he rubbed his red ringed eyes fruitlessly and continually readjusted his glasses. A woman stood leaning forward on a table, her head propped up precariously on her hands.
In front of him was another man, evidently the one who had been shouting frantically moments before. His glasses sat crooked on his nose, and his shoulders were bowed heavily forward — yet he could not have been older than thirty years of age. He stood panting, exhausted from his outburst, and struggled even to lift his head enough to face the man who had just walked in.
“Who are you?” He said.
“Sam Miller, you let me in five minutes ago.”
“I did?” The dishevelled man trundled over to the nearby table and checked his phone, “Oh, so I did,” he looked Sam up and down, “right, you’ve just flown in from—?”
“California.”
“Long way to come,” he rubbed his eyes, “sorry, I’m Dr. Browne. My colleagues are Dr. —, Dr. —, and Dr. Caldera.” He gestured to each of the other researchers: the first man, the second man, and the woman respectively. “I’m sure you heard my outburst earlier — which I do apologise for — but as you can see we’re really at our ends right now.”
“Well I didn’t fly eleven hours to come out of this empty handed. Have you got any kind of neural network set up already?”
Dr. Caldera joined the conversation, “yes, but it’s quite rudimentary and hasn’t given us any meaningful analyses at all.”
“How quickly do you think you can run me through your API?”
“An hour, perhaps.” Dr. Caldera replied.
“Then let’s get to it.”

The night bus was packed with passengers. All of them were quiet, most of them scrolling on their phones, some looking blankly out the windows — lost entirely within their own thoughts.
A series of large white banners showcased various devices back-dropped with subtle purple lights. They were hung up on the old residential flats, strewn over the windows below — blocking out the sunlight for all but the top rooms. The old sleeping bankers now laid in the ground in a cemetery some distance from the city.
“East Campus Street!” The driver called out as he pulled up to the stop.
The passengers flooded out from the bus. He remembered the day — almost a decade ago now — when a strange man with a heavy suitcase tipped him double the price of the ticket to be taken to this very street. He remembered how that man disappeared into the cold white building at the same hour as the passengers he was transporting now.
Nine years ago that building showed hardly a single sign of life inside; today its frosted windows glowed brightly over the streets below.
The bus emptied and the driver set off once more, ready to pick up the next round of passengers sure to be waiting throughout the city, and in his wake he left one of the many small mobs that were to travel to that street each night.
A crowd of weary peoples, waddling into the central consumer facility of the corporation known as Ever-Lucid.
