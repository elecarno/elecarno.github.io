The drive was a blur, headlights and streetlamps lurched out from the darkness then faded back into the night. The whole trip was poorly recollected. K attempted to piece the fragments together, twisting and stretching Mitchell’s mind.
Sounds overlapped, the sky flashed. Memories bled into one another, spilling together until it all came to a sudden halt. He was stood by a battered car, stuck in the mud deep in the forest. Toby and Elise were rummaging through the boot.
Mitchell was shoved from behind.
“Continue,” K ordered.
Elise moved from the back of the car and approached him. In her arms she cradled a wooden rifle. The carved stock curved gently upwards and gave way to a long black barrel, topped with a large magnified scope.
“This was Siobhan’s,” she handed him the weapon, “don’t you dare miss that shot.”
Toby joined them, a pair of bolt-cutters hanging from his hands. He gave them only a nod, and the team moved into action.

Mitchell followed Toby through the woods while Elise moved in the opposite direction. The ground grew steeper, and soon moonlight could be seen streaking through the trees as they reached the edge of the treeline. Toby stopped at the threshold, ducking behind the tall grass ahead of them. They were soon to be at the top of the hill, upon which stood a small metal shack with a signal tower nearby.
He scanned the area. “I don’t see much security, they’re not expecting anyone to approach. You could check the building a bit better with that scope.”
“Well who would approach a signal tower?” Mitchell pulled the rifle down from his shoulder and held it up, placing his eye to the scope.
“Exactly, and besides, ‘the bigger they are…’” Toby chuckled.
“I think I see a guard at the door, doesn’t looked armed though.”
“We can deal with him. Let’s go.”

They split up and moved slowly through the grass, each arcing up and towards the building. A light wind glided over the hillside, the movement of the grass masking their trails.
Mitchell was almost at the building when he heard a sudden clatter. He lifted his head slightly and watched as Toby leapt from the grass and ensnared the guard. A black cloth quickly engulfed the man’s face as Toby pulled it taut behind his neck, slowly bringing him to the ground.
Toby looked around before gesturing for Mitchell to approach. They moved to the door. Toby brought the bolt-cutters to bear and clamped them down onto the padlock, cutting clean through.
The door swung open and they rushed inside. Large monitors lined the wall from end to end. Dozens of graphs and meters, a column of patient files, and a map of the town in the valley below. A lone man was stationed at the desk. He swung to face them in shock.
Mitchell raised the gun, “don’t move.”
Toby ran over to the man and questioned him, “where’s the alarm?”
“What?” the man replied.
“How do I trip the emergency alarm that calls in the company?”
The man pointed to a small covered button on the underside of the desk.
“Thanks.” Toby said before promptly knocking him out with the back of the cutters.
On the right side of the room was a raised bed not dissimilar to those Mitchell had formerly used in the EverLucid booths, yet this was less refined — with sharp corners and visible welds, and a bulky headset with dozens of thick cables protruding up to a box bolted to the ceiling.
Toby climbed onto the bed, “alright, strap me in.”
Mitchell lowered the headset onto him and tightened it accordingly.
“It’s been six years since I was in one of these.”
“Twice is a lot better than my hundreds.”
“Once was always too much.”
Mitchell walked over to the nearby pedestal and activated the screen. He flicked through  the buttons until finding a long list of parameters. “What did Siobhan say we needed again?”
“The ‘depth’ setting mostly. Just crank it all though, I’d like to go out with some style.”
Mitchell turned each parameter up to its maximum value. “You ready?”
“Hit it, I’ll see you on the ‘flip-side’ or whatever they say.”
He activated the headset. A blinding flash emanated from beneath the visor. Toby screamed out and his body writhed, before just as quickly he fell back to the bed completely limp.

Mitchell turned to the desk and the wall of screens emanating their blue glow upon him. A large map of the village below covered the centre screen — with a status marker on each house. To the left, dozens of panels corresponded with each of the markers. The panels were labelled with various terms to do with M.C.E.T. technologies.
A few of the status makers had turned red. He watched as another one flicked from green to crimson, and another. To the right — a wall of camera feeds displaying the surveillance placed throughout the village. A bright flash appeared on one of the feeds, and he saw Elise standing at the foot of a church tower, a flare in her hand. A small crowd of dazed onlookers was starting to form around her.
He moved to the keyboard and began manipulating the interface. He searched its menus, trawling through the cryptic options. He turned off every controllable technology he could find, until eventually every house on the map was red, and the numbers in the crowd grew.
Mitchell slammed his hand against the alarm underneath the desk, and exited the shack — taking one final glance at Toby’s lifeless body before shutting the door behind him.

The wind had grown stronger atop the hill. He found a rock jutting out from the grass and laid himself upon it. Numerous windows were now lit across the village, and a plume of red smoke rose from the church.
Taking the rifle down from his shoulder, Mitchell put his eye to the scope and peered down upon the village centre where Elise stood. Through the small and warped lens he could make out the movement of her mouth as she made her speech to the gathering village folk.
He looked up from the scope and towards the night sky. He watched as the dark clouds rolled past, and strained his eyes to catch every glimpse he could of the stars beyond them.
“It’s almost perfect isn’t it?” A voice suddenly appeared beside him.
Mitchell nearly fell from the rock. He instantly felt the hazy feeling of his dreams creep back in. He turned to see K standing in the grass next to him.
“It turns out,” K continued, “that the final ingredient to creating a dream that is truly indistinguishable from reality is simply removing all knowledge that one is within a dream.” The world was frozen around them. “That includes subconscious retention of course. Throughout this interrogation we’ve been pulling the strings in your mind quite a bit — the moment I’m gone we’ll remove any and all information about me and you’ll be back to re-experiencing these moments of your life. When I return as I am doing now, we need only reintroduce the information of my existence and the brain is able to adapt quite readily.”
Mitchell realised he could not move.
“It’s a shame we cannot yet put the process to market. It has some rather significant side effects — akin to a lobotomy. There are some… similarly apparent gaps in your recollection already however. A few possibilities present themselves: the first, is that your mind was simply deteriorating already. After your thousands of hours in our headsets it would hardly be a surprise. The second, is that our tampering has been more severe than intended; but I highly doubt that — our technicians are very experienced.” He turned to the shack behind them. “The final option, is that by some manner — likely Siobhan — you discovered this capability beforehand and have already utilised it, would that be so?”
Mitchell could not speak.
K chuckled. “Do not worry, were it so you would have no recollection anyway.” He walked away, disappearing into the grass.

The low whirr of a helicopter’s blades could be heard rapidly approaching in the distance. Mitchell pressed the rifle to his cheek and looked down the scope once more. The flare in Elise’s hand was beginning to die down, yet crowds around her had grown vocal, and their chanting could be heard down the valley.
He waited until the helicopter was nearly overhead, the grass swaying wildly beneath it. Carefully he lined up the cross on the scope to cover Elise’s head, moved it a few notches to up and to the left to account for wind and distance — and fired into the night.
