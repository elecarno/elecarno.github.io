The sun hung low over the city as Ralph walked down its narrow cobblestone streets, a long shadow cast ahead of him. He ascended a stairway, and the low rumble of an explosion in the distance rushed through the air. The townspeople came rushing from their doors to view the source of the disturbance.
Only a dozen miles from the city walls, a plume of smoke could be seen rising from a nearby village — the signature blue flare of the revolution climbing alongside it. Gasps of horror could be heard amongst the crowds, and a loud horn was sounded to signal the beginning of curfew. The city was under siege.

Ralph opened the front door to his home and stepped inside to see his father looming over him. He had walked at a leisurely pace while the masses rushed around him.
“Where have you been?” his father inquired angrily.
“I went for a walk.”
“A walk? We are at war! The enemy will be at our gates before sunset. You’re coming with me to the command bunker.”
“No I’m not.”
“What do you mean you’re not?”
“I’m staying here.”
The two argued for some time, yet Ralph could not be swayed. His father was a General in the King’s army, and duty called.

With his father departed, Ralph spared no time in removing the royal lapel on his collar. He ran up the stairs to his room and retrieved a box hidden under his bed. Inside were a few books banned by the King, and a blue armband emblazoned with the symbol of the revolution.
He was about to put the armband on when he glanced up at the photograph of his mother framed atop his bedside cabinet. He decided to tear the armband down the middle, leaving only half of the symbol remaining before putting it on.

***

Planes flew overhead and bombs rained down upon the buildings. The sky erupted with the constant bursts of flak and shells.
Ralph lifted a chair with both arms and hurled it at the small window in his room. He crawled through the shattered glass and sat upon the slanted roof as the fiery light of the sunset washed over him. Towards the centre of the city was the Royal Palace. Ralph watched the artillery placed atop its tall white walls fire shells that arced through the air and came crashing down on the invading force.
It wasn’t long until the city defences were breached. Using a pair of binoculars he had stolen from his father he watched the revolutionaries flood through the gates and down the city streets while the King’s soldiers scrambled to maintain a perimeter.
Blue flares rose across the city. Guerrilla fighters emerged from hiding and former loyalists defected to the new oncoming authority.
A loud banging came from downstairs, then the clatter of a wooden door being smashed open.
Ralph glanced down at his torn armband as he heard footsteps come rushing up the stairway and the cold barrel of a rifle was pressed against the back of his head.

“Turn around, slowly.”
Ralph stepped down from the roof and backed in through the smashed window. He turned to face the soldier.
The soldier inspected Ralph. He could see the boy was well dressed, with cotton trousers and a black cardigan atop an off-white shirt. To the soldier, it was the stereotypical dress of the loyalist — an everyday prison culturally imposed by the regime. He saw however, the absence of the royal lapel, and the presence of the torn armband.
“Why is your armband torn?”
Ralph looked at the soldier. He was young, wearing a simple uniform that was clearly once well made but now tattered. He had large bags under his sunken eyes and a scar across his lip. “My mother was killed by one of your attacks.”
The soldier’s grip on his rifle faltered, the barrel drooped momentarily. He was panting heavily, and flinched at the sound of every explosion outside. Ralph had no such reaction.
“Who do you support?”
“Not the king.”
“And the revolution?”
Ralph sighed, unbothered by the barrel pointed at his face. He lowered his arms and turned back to step out onto the roof again.
“Hey! what are you doing?” the soldier raised the rifle as if to aim, yet his hands shuddered.
“You’re not going to shoot me.”
“Yes I will!” The gun was now visibly shaking.
“No you won’t — why did you sign up to fight?”
The soldier thought of making further threats, but decided to answer instead. “My parents were farmers, the King taxed our crops during the last drought. My parents fed me the little food we had before they starved to death themselves.”
“So you’re fighting for their memory.”
“I’m fighting so no one else has to bury their parents like I did.”
“Sure.”
“What do you mean ‘sure’?” he steadied his grip on the rifle and pressed it into Ralph once more.
“I’ll shoot! I swear I’ll shoot! Why are you even on the roof? Where’s your father? Did we get him too? Was he a loyalist?”
“Look, how old are you? What’s your name?”
The doubt crept back into the soldier’s mind, again his weapon began to shake. “I’m seventeen, my name’s Terry, what does that matter?”
“I’m sixteen, Terry! Sixteen! My name is Ralph, we could have been friends in a school together — but no, here we are pointing guns and shouting at each other and calling it the fight for freedom!”
Terry saw for the first time the youthfulness of the face in front of him. He dropped his rifle. An explosion shook the building, yet the two boys stood unshaken and stared at each other in silence. Eventually Ralph turned again and sat back down on the roof.
“Can I sit with you?” Terry asked. Ralph gave him a weak nod and he crawled through the broken window to sit next to him. They sat together on the red tiles with tucked knees as a soft breeze blew through their hair. Gunshots echoed below. From the roof it seemed almost like a normal day, but with gunfire instead of crowds.
“My father is a General in the King’s army.” Ralph muttered.
“Is he tough?” Terry muttered back.
“Well, he couldn’t convince me to go to the bunker with him.”
“And?”
“And I think the city will fall.”
They sat in silence. A plane crashed nearby, a few houses went up in flames.

The battle waxed and waned as both sides played their games of grand strategy. One army would retreat and the other would flood in to take their place, only to be gunned down by an ambush. The skies grew quiet again, as craters and flames now covered the city — the walls of the Royal Palace cracked and crumbling.
“We never had much fuel, it was hard to keep the fight going.”
“You never wrote enough — and we never read enough.”
Terry looked at him confused. Ralph shuffled back through the window, and returned a few moments later with a book. He handed it to Terry as he sat back down on the roof. It was dark now, and quiet once again. The skies had cleared and moonlight glowed over the city.
The book was old, the symbol of the revolution adorned its cover. Terry opened to the first page, the language was archaic and difficult to read, yet as he worked down the opening preface he soon saw that the book was dated to nearly a hundred years ago.
“I’ve never heard of this?”
“It’s banned. That’s probably one of the only copies left.”
Terry gestured to return the book, yet Ralph refused.
“It doesn't matter if the revolution wins, it doesn't matter if the whole royal family is slaughtered. One day someone is always going to be fighting for freedom again.” A flare rose in the distance. “We’ve already been fighting for as long as we can possibly know.” The artillery resumed firing. “I know I’m just some kid, but I don’t see how that fight is going to end here with some gunpowder.” Soldiers began pouring out of bunkers and into the streets, gunfire was exchanged once more. “I don’t think that’s freedom, I can’t see it; and I can’t understand why everyone fighting in this city thinks it is.”
“So what is then? How do we win?”
“We can’t ‘win’ — there is no ‘win’. Not in my mind and not in those minds a hundred years ago. We have to make freedom, we have to make it and keep it alive.”
Voices could be heard below.
“I wanted to flee once, but I couldn’t make my freedom in time.”
A gunshot rang out from directly beneath them.
“Now I’m sitting on the roof — the most ‘free’ I’ve ever been —”
Footsteps came rushing up the stairs.
“—and I guess it might be too late.”
Once more, a rifle at the back of his head.
